Corporate Bonds are having a Goldilocks moment

In our last post, we illustrated that despite high cash rates, investors would have historically been rewarded by investing in corporate fixed income rather than cash near the end of a central bank hiking cycle. This raised the obvious question – if you are being rewarded for taking risk, why not take more and allocate to non-investment grade (high yield) bonds?

Since 1980, the US Federal Reserve has undertaken seven significant rate hiking cycles: (July 1980 to May 1981), (February 1983 to August 1984), (February 1988 to March 1989), (January 1994 to February 1995), (May 1999 to May 2000), (May 2004 to June 2006) and (November 2015 to December 2018), averaging 19 months in length and a 4.2% increase in interest rates.

When looking at the 12-month forward returns of investment grade corporate bonds versus their high-yield counterparts at different points of the US Federal Reserve’s tightening cycle, a very clear pattern emerges. In the early part of a hiking cycle (24 to 12 months before the pause), high yield bonds outperform their investment-grade counterparts, which is logical when you consider the reason central banks hike interest rates is to slow strong economic growth and inflationary pressures.

During the period between one year before and one year after the pause in the hiking cycle high yield bonds have historically underperformed their investment grade counterparts. Again, this makes sense as the central bank will be pausing rate hikes because the economy is slowing, and inflation is under control. It stands to reason that a slowing economy and higher interest rate costs as a result of tightening are less favorable conditions for more indebted and riskier businesses.

The Fed is normally not ‘on pause’ for a very long period of time, and markets are currently expecting the central bank to begin cutting rates again in 2024. It is not until at least 1.5 years after the end of the hiking cycle that high-yield bonds have historically outperformed their investment grade counterparts, and the benefits of easing economic conditions are reflected in riskier bond returns.

If history is a guide, corporate bonds are currently experiencing a ‘Goldilocks’ like just right mix of investment risk at this stage of the cycle – not too hot and not too cool.
