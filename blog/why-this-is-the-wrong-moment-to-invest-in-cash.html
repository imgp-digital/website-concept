Why this is the wrong moment to invest in cash

With central banks nearing the end of their hiking cycles and base rates approaching their highest yields in over 20 years, investors could be tempted to take their money out of fixed income markets and invest in cash. If history is a guide, this would be the wrong decision.

Since 1980, the US Federal Reserve has undertaken 7 significant rate hiking cycles (Jul '80 to May ’81, Feb '83 to Aug '84, Feb '88 to Mar '89, Jan '94 to Feb '95, May '99 to May '00, May '04 to Jun '06 and Nov '15 toDec '18), averaging 19 months in length and a 4.2% increase in interest rates. The current tightening cycle, which began in March 2022 with a Fed Funds rate of -0.25% and potentially finishing in July 2023 with the Federal Reserve rate’s upper bound set at 5.5% has been average in length (18 months), however has exhibited a quicker pace of tightening (5.25% increase in total).

We have analyzed the 12-month forward returns at different points of the US Federal Reserve’s tightening cycle, and while it would have been better for investors to reallocate to cash before the central bank reached its peak rate, from approximately 4 months before to 12 months after the Fed pauses, bonds have outperformed cash by a significant margin of between 4 to 8% on a one year forward basis.

The historical outperformance of fixed income versus cash at this point cycle is a result of yields declining after the Fed stops hiking (when fixed income yields decline, prices increase). The Fed is normally not ‘on pause’ for a very long period of time, and markets are currently pricing in cuts in the Fed Funds rate beginning in January 2024.

Whilst it may be tempting for investors to keep money on the sidelines in cash, history shows us that fortune favours the brave, and excess returns have been realized by allocating to fixed income at this point in the cycle.
